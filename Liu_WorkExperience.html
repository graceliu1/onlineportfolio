Work Experience Narrative


As a final-year student in the Engineering and Design Academy at Dublin High, I have met many industry professionals through workplace field trips and guest speakers. These experiences have provided a valuable preview into different fields of engineering. Having the opportunity to ask questions and see the environment of these professionals has been one of the most rewarding parts of my high school journey so far.

~field trips~

The first field trip I participated in was Manufacturing Day - a visit with students from around the Tri-Valley to several companies. The company that I visited was Kensington Laboratories. We were able to tour the companies and see their primary products. When we arrived at Kensington Laboratories, we met with several general managers to take a tour of their facility. They specialized in wafer-handling robots, from their manufacture to repairs. I learned that wafers are flat silicon disks that serve as semiconductors for circuits. They can be cut apart to form small computer chips that are sent to other major companies like Intel for processing.

The second virtual field trip I attended was the Junior Achievement Virtual Job Shadow with Oracle in 2020. I attended this job shadow to learn about people at Oracle's experiences with their career. One presenter was Gloria Cheng. I remember learning about her graduation from UC Berkeley and subsequent work experience at IBM and then Oracle. Hearing about her progression as a women in STEM was interesting because I participated in the Girls Who Code Club myself. Overall, her talk was very inspiring.

The third field trip I participated in was the Junior Achievement Virtual Job Shadow with Salesforce in 2020. I attended this job shadow to learn about people at Salesforce's daily work roles. I listened to several individuals narrate their experiences working in the technology after graduating from schools like the University of Southern Carolina. Overall, this inspired me to consider alternative paths to a career in tech companies besides obtaining a bachelor's in computer science. Individuals who minor or major in the humanities in undergrad can also take on roles as well.

The last field trip I attended was the CTE webinars. Specifically, the two I attended were the webinars on Biotechnology and Teaching. The Biotechnology webinar featured students from Diablo Valley College discussing their experiences in the intersection of engineering and biology. They talked about their college professors and courses that allowed them to gain practical lab experience, even at laboratories like Sandia National Labs. The Teaching webinar was on teaching as a possible path. Though I've always wanted to work in a software company, I think that learning about why California needs more teachers helped educate me on the importance of guiding young minds.

~guest speakers~

One of the guest speakers that visited my Principles of Engineering classroom was Jason Yosinski, a machine learning scientist who had previously worked in Uber’s AI Labs. He explained some of his research relating to neural networks and also focused on a few projects he worked on in college. For example, he built a spider-like robot that used machine learning and neural networks to figure out the best way to walk. I find it fascinating how by studying enormous amounts of text found on the Internet, models can generate blocks of text that are logical coming from a computer system.

Another guest speaker talk that I attended was virtually with Emma Schoenthal, a civil engineer who graduated from UCSD and talked about her experiences choosing her career path. I learned that not immediately knowing your path entering college is a common issue that many students face. While exploring classes at UCSD and taking internships at different environmntal technology companies, Ms. Schoenthal was able to decide on her career path. I thought that her lecture was very interesting because she incorporated the perspective of a previous Dublin High School student.

My third guest speaker event also occurred virtually with Andrea Madotto, who was a PhD candidate at The Hong Kong University of Science and Technology (HKUST) and another natural language processing scientist. Like Jason Yoskinski, he had extensive experience with modelling conversations and working on generative language models. I thought that his research on the latest attempts to simulate AI and human speech patterns was especially interesting. Later in my AP Computer Science Applications class, I worked on creating a simple chatbot. It was enlightening to hear about the cutting-edge research being conducted on natural language processing.

Kimberli Zhong was the final guest speaker at a talk I attended in my senior year. Her focus was on her experiences in high school, to her schooling at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT), to her career path. I got to hear about how she participated in the first cohort of the Engineering and Design Academy as well as her role in the Gael Force Robotics. Like Ms. Schoenthal, hearing from people who attended Dublin High always provides an unique perspective. Finally, I learned about her experience finding summer internships in companies like AirBnb and Google.

As a junior, I also participated in the mentorship program with an industry professional. My assigned mentor was James Morehead, a current product manager at Google. Through our monthly mentor meetings, I received much beneficial advice surrounding college and pursuing a career in the software industry. For example, one of the meetings I learned the most from was the mock job interview. I greatly appreciated listening to advice on remaining calm and thinking through curveball questions. I also learned how to present myself and my experiences by telling short stories. This prepared me for several interviews for summer programs. Another meaningful experience was discussing future plans for college. Discussions with my mentor helped put college into perspective. I look forward to implementing these lessons in my life and career.
